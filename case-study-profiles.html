Historic growth and decline

Population growth since 1801

Between 1921 and 2001 Oldham experienced a decline in its total population, and is still 35,000 below its historic peak. By contrast, the population in the other three local authority areas has increased more or less continuously since 1801, with Oxford rapidly recovering from a population drop in the 1970s.

Temporary blips in the data are likely due to an artefact of the way the historic data has been collated.

Manufacturing employment since 1841

The chart below shows the percentage of residents employed in manufacturing since 1841; the black lines are all (modern) local authority areas in Great Britain. Between 1841 and 1951 around 70% of residents in work in Oldham were employed in manufacturing, since when there has been a steep decline. Only Stockport, Dundee and Preston have had larger percentage point falls in manufacturing employment. Although manufacturing employment in Oxford also fell steeply, it was a relative newcomer to the economy and was a smaller proportion of employment at its peak (~40%).

Crime

Recorded violent crime

Recorded violent crime has seen a sharp rise, particularly in Oldham and Thanet, though recording of violent crime has been subject to recent changes…

Hospital admissions for violence

Demography

Recent population growth

The populations of all local authority areas have grown since 2001. Although it is not apparent on this chart, the population of Oldham has grown at a slower rate than the other three local authority areas.

Age structure

The four areas have very distinctive age profiles. Oxford is dominated by its student population; Oldham has a larger than average child population; Thatnet has an under-representation of 20-40 year-olds and an over-representation of over-60s; Tunbridge Wells ha a smaller than average population of 20-40 year-olds and a larger than average population of 40-60 year-olds.

2011 Census maps: Margate | Oldham | Oxford | Tunbridge Wells

Ethnic group

People of White British ethnic group made up 90% of the population of Thanet and Tunbridge Wells areas in 2011. They made up smaller proportions of the population in Oldham and Oxford, which had large populations of people of Bangladeshi or Pakistani and ‘Other White’ ethnic origins respectively.

2011 Census maps: Margate | Oldham | Oxford | Tunbridge Wells

Household type

By household type:

  • Oldham’s profile is similar to the England average, with a higher than average proportion of households with children, and lone parent households in particular
  • Oxford has a larger than average proportion of single person and multi-person households (the latter likely to be houses in multiple occupation)
  • Thanet has a larger than average proportion of single person households
  • Tunbridge Wells is similar to the national average, with a larger than average proportion of married couples with children

Economic indicators

Economic output

Gross valued added (GVA) is a measure of the output of economic activity that takes place in an area, analogous to national GDP. This plot of GVA shows that the impact of the recession on economic output in Oxford and Tunbridge Wells was very limited. Output in Oldham reduced sharply but recovered; in Thanet, economic output has barely grown since the recession.

Unemployment

Following the recession, unemployment rose to a much greater extent in Oldham and Thanet that in Oxford and Tunbridge Wells.

2011 Census maps: Margate | Oldham | Oxford | Tunbridge Wells

Household income

Average gross household income per head is around two-thirds higher in Tunbridge Wells and Oxford compared to Thanet and Oldham. Households in Oldham and Thanet are estimated to have diverged in their average incomes since 1997, with Thanet faring better.

Housing

Tenure

Rates of owner occupation in 2011 in Oldham, Thanet and Tunbridge Wells were very similar to the national average. They differ with respect to the composition of the rental sector - more social housing than private rented in Oldham, the reverse in Thanet, with roughly equal numbers in Tunbridge Wells. In Oxford a majority of households lived in the rental sector, a larger proportion in private rented housing.

2011 Census maps: Margate | Oldham | Oxford | Tunbridge Wells

House prices

House prices in Oldham and Thanet were very similar in 1995, but have diverged significantly since - the median house price in 2017 being 127,000 and 220,000 respectively. House prices in Oldham have risen very slowly since the recession. House price growth in Tunbridge Wells and Oxford slowed after the recession but has picked up strongly since around 2013.

Rough sleeping

These estimates of rough sleeping are a ‘snapshot’ count of the number of people esimated to be sleeping rough on a single night in October or November. Counts are conducted by local authorities - some physically go out and count people on the street, others simply provide an estimate based on local intelligence. Therefore these numbers - and comparison between areas - are to be treated with some caution.

Using this method, the number of people sleeping rough has risen sharply in Oxford and Thanet since 2010, whilst in Oldham and Tunbridge Wells the numbers have changed little over the period.

Health

Detailed health profiles: Thanet | Oldham | Oxford | Tunbridge Wells

Life expectancy

In 2014-16 female life expectancy was higher than the national average in Oxford and Tunbridge Wells, and lower than the national average in Oldham and Thanet local authority areas. There is evidence of a recent fall in life expectancy in all areas except Oxford, in line with recent trends. Male life expectancy has not shown the same evidence of decline in these areas.

Teenaged pregnancy

The teenaged pregnancy rate has fallen sharply across the country, including in the case study areas. Neverthless it remains just above the England average in Oldham and Thanet, despite absolute falls over the period. Oxford had a rate above the national average in the early 2000s but it had fallen below the national average by 2016.

Infant mortality

Infant mortality rates fell nationally over the period, though recently there has been a small increase. Most of the cases study areas were not significantly different to the England average in 2015-17, except Oldham which had a significantly higher rate.

Deaths from drug misuse

Deaths attributable from drug misuse are typically higher in Oxford and Thanet compared to the England average. There is some evidence that the death rate in Oldham has increased in recent years.

Suicide

Suicide rates are highly variable from year to year, but broadly speaking do not show any statistically significant difference to the England average (which has remained constant over the period since 2001-03).

Labour demand

Job density

‘Job density’ is the ratio of the number of jobs to the size of the working age population. In Oxford there are more jobs than working-age residents, which reflects its function as a location of employment for workers in the wider Oxfordshire area. Oldham and Thanet have around two jobs for every three working age residents, meaning many residents must commute elsewhere for employment opportunities. This is also true - to a lesser extent - for Tunbridge Wells.

Jobs by industry

This chart shows the number of jobs based in the area by type of industry (by the ‘Standard Industrial Classification’).

  • Oldham still has a larger than average number of jobs in manufacturing, with wholesale, retail, transportation and storage industries also providing a larger than average number of jobs.
  • Oxford, by contrast, now has a small than average proportion of jobs in manufacturing despite the continued existence of the car factory; employment now is dominated by the universities and hospitals.
  • Jobs in Thanet are disproportionately in ‘arts, entertainment and recreation’, ‘accommodation and food services’ - presumably due to tourism - and health and education.
  • Tunbridge Wells has a larger than average number of jobs in ‘finance and insurance’, being home to the offices of several insurance companies.

Low paid jobs

Thanet and Oldham were estimated in 2014 to have around one-third of jobs paid below the living wage (as calculated by the Living Wage Foundation).

Labour supply

Economic activity rates

People of working age can be classified as either economically active (engaged in paid work, or looking for it) or economically inactive. Men and women in Oxford and Tunbridge Wells both have slightly higher than average economic activity rates, whereas Thanet rates around the national average. Oldham has economic activity rates below the national average, particularly for women.

2011 Census maps: Margate | Oldham | Oxford | Tunbridge Wells

Reasons for economic inactivity

A number of reasons can be given for economic inactivity i.e. not engaging in the labour market. The chart below shows estimates from the Annual Population Survey. The number of responses is sometimes too small for a reliable estimate, which is why some values are missing.

  • Oldham’s high economically inactive rates are driven by a larger than average number of people engaged in domestic rather than paid labour, along with a slightly higher than average proportion of people long-term sick.
  • Thanet has economic inactivity rates around the national average, but an unusually large number of these people are experiencing long-term sickness.
  • Oxford has a large number of students, as expected, but also a large proportion of inactivity due to domestic responsibilities.
  • Tunbridge Wells has larger than average proportion of inactivity due to early retirement.

2011 Census maps: Margate | Oldham | Oxford | Tunbridge Wells

Occupation

Oldham residents are more likely than average to be employed in ‘elementary’ occupations (e.g. cleaning, security, packing, sales assistants). Thanet residents are more likely than average to be employed in caring, leisure, or skilled trade occupations. Employed residents in Oxford and Tunbridge Wells are more likely than average to be employed in professional occupations, particularly in Oxford where this accounts for nearly four in ten employed residents.

2011 Census maps: Margate | Oldham | Oxford | Tunbridge Wells

Qualifications

The number of residents qualified to degree level or above is much higher than the national average in Oxford, similar to average in Tunbridge Wells, and below average in Oldham and Thanet. Qualification levels can be heavily influenced by the age profile of residents.

2011 Census maps: Margate | Oldham | Oxford | Tunbridge Wells

Child poverty

The measure of child poverty presented here is based upon claims for in-work tax credits and out-of-work benefits. It estimates the number of children living in families with incomes below 60% of median income (before housing costs). It does not include claims registered under Universal Credit which began to be rolled out in 2013 and may explain some of the falls observed here. This measure has been discontinued since 2015.

These caveats aside, the measure shows that the child poverty rate in 2015 was significantly higher than the England average in both Thanet and Oldham. It was similar to the national average in Oxford, and well below the national average in Tunbridge Wells.

Working age benefit claims

The proportion of working age people claiming employment and support allowance or incapacity benefit - paid to people who are unable to work due to long-term ill-health or disability - is much higher than average in Oldham and Thanet. They also have larger proportions of people claiming out of work benefits due to lone parenthood, unemployment, disability or caring responsibilities. The proportion of people claiming out of work benefits is lower than average in Oxford and Tunbridge Wells across all of these categories.
